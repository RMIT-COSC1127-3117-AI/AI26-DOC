## 📋 Course Details

### Course Description

This course introduces you to the basic concepts and techniques of Artificial Intelligence (AI). AI is the sub-area of computer science devoted to creating software and hardware that get computers to do things that would be considered "intelligent" if people did them. Artificial intelligence has had an active and exciting history and is now a reasonably mature area of computer science. Many of its research discoveries have reached the point of industrial application, and many companies have made and saved millions of dollars by exploiting the results of AI research. However, the goal of emulating human intelligence has not been reached, and many stimulating and challenging problems remain.

All serious programmers and software engineers should know the major AI techniques, which are regarded by many as core knowledge for any Computer Science degree. This course will help you gain generic problem-solving skills applicable to a wide range of real-world problems. Topics covered include search strategies for solving problems, knowledge representation, automated planning, intelligent agents, reasoning under uncertainty, bio-inspired optimisation, and machine learning.

What makes AI unlike most other areas of Computer Science is that it does not live in a purely formal, exact world of provably correct algorithms; it constantly reaches into "softer", less exact fields such as philosophy, ethics, psychology, cognitive science, linguistics, biology, and even economics. Building a system that reasons, plans, or learns forces us to ask questions that computer science alone cannot answer: 

* What does it mean to know something?
* What is a good decision under uncertainty?
* Is a behaviour intelligent, or just a convincing imitation of intelligence?
* Who is responsible when an autonomous system gets it wrong? 

This is precisely what makes AI so fun and rewarding to study: it sits at the crossroads of engineering rigour and big, open-ended questions about minds, values, and society, and progress in the field genuinely depends on borrowing insights from these other disciplines. Being comfortable moving between hard technical content and these broader, messier questions is itself a valuable and increasingly sought-after skill. 👍

### Learning Objectives

In short: by the end of this course you should understand what AI actually is and how it fits into Computer Science, know the main techniques in the AI toolbox (search, knowledge representation, planning, agents, machine learning, and reasoning under uncertainty), be able to pick the right technique for a given problem and actually build a working solution with it, understand the trade-offs (speed, accuracy, complexity) between different approaches, and be able to talk about all of this using the field's proper terminology.

The course learning objectives (CLOs) are:

- **CLO 1**: describe the key components of the artificial intelligence (AI) field and its relation and role in Computer Science;
- **CLO 2**: identify and describe artificial intelligence techniques, including search heuristics, knowledge representation, automated planning and agent systems, machine learning, and probabilistic reasoning;
- **CLO 3**: identify and apply AI techniques to a wide range of problems, including complex problem solving via search, knowledge-base systems, machine learning, probabilistic models, agent decision making, etc.;
- **CLO 4**: design and implement appropriate AI solution techniques for such problems;
- **CLO 5**: analyse and understand the computational trade-offs involved in applying different AI techniques and models.
- **CLO 6**: communicate clearly and effectively using the technical language of the field correctly.

#### Assumed Knowledge

Algorithms and Analysis itself comes after significant programming and mathematical training. Thus, you are expected to have passed those courses and have well-developed programming skills (at least two programming courses) and knowledge of basic discrete mathematics (e.g., sets, functions, relations, propositional logic).

If you want to refresh your maths, a perfect (free!) book is [The Book of Proofs](https://richardhammack.github.io/BookOfProof/) 📖 . The whole book is important and relevant for the AI course. The only Chapter that is not relevant is Chapter 13 (Proofs on Calculus).
